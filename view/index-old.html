Starting from scratch. Technology shouldn't handicap. Honest narrative beats anything. Design to understand. Regarding Education.

Philosophy

Look at the entirety of a project, line by line. How much of it is understood clearly?

Interactive designers and developers seem obsessively motivated to supply a bundle or framework for every situation imaginable. Akin to creating heaps and heaps one-note instruments (or the instrument to end all instruments) instead of new music. In all seriousness, I don't think any creative besides a software developer believes they can so easily solve problems with a single master-stroke and a very general solution.

I will try to counter this ranting with a little jazz of my own; the following projects are less 300kb total and started from scratch. When talking kb, I mean all files that compose a project from the backend systems to the frontend templates, scripts, stylesheets, etc.. I'm not saying projects should be small, rather, I propose that we develop more projects on purpose. I am focusing on the 'backend' work I've pursued over the last few years, as those decisions define are most often invisible, and thus taken for granted.

Sound Opinions

2013

soundopinions.org

Sound opinions is a nationally syndicated radio program and podcast.

Respect the Syntax

It is perhaps no big surprise that the producers of Sound Opinions adhere to NPR affiliate cliché of having a firm appreciation for egg-headedness. When approaching an organization as a collection capable humans, one should take the opportunity to design for that organization. In my case, I pursue designs that rely as heavily as they can on the human end of the bargain, though this can only be accomplished by placing firm expectations on that same end. And a wee note, whenever I say the word 'design' in the context of anything I do, I care little for the arrangment of items and layouts—I'm referring to the action one takes to perform a task and realize a goal.

The inherited content from the old site had been crafted; artists, albums, places, etc. had been noted meticulously. The site they were preparing to leave behind was 100% old-school html—a format I truly and deeply value. The riddle was thus: how do you keep the uniqueness of custom authored markup but enhance it with a management system that makes creating new content, adding metadata, and searching and sorting content easy (for syntax enthusiasts, of course). Given that the goal is to end with markup, why not just start with markup? Of course, that means putting all your trust in the human...

Outcome

I prototyped a single-text-box cms to show the possibility of using only syntax to create episodes—an idea that, quite naturally, was met with suspicion. That quick prototype turned out convincing enough, and interestingly, that work never changed all that much in the final production version.

A user simply creates an outline like they would in a word processing program. For complicated endeavors, like media-management, syntax was provided to work with every api imaginable: soundcloud, youtube, flickr and iTunes. Copy a linkt from YouTube and you have a player, or to Flickr and you have a gallery.

For a site to provide context and navigability an admin provides metadata,—usually in a series of menus, checkboxes and other inputs. In observing people fill out forms for a decade, I've never gotten the impression that it is an enjoyable activity. But metadata can be enjoyable if it saves you time, especially if you already did the hard parts of authoring some content. In a nutshell, the Sound Opinions CMS tracks metadata by asking that an admin to look over the copy and highlight significant elements (like an album or artist name). They can link the bits of highlighted text to one another by literally drawing a line between them. Some patterns have meaning—connecting an album and and artist triggers a query to find and embed album art. I daresay, it's almost fun.

Metadata Graphing

12/31/15

Video Presentation

The Knight Prototype Fund is a grant to encourage and share new ideas and expose the results to a wider audience.

Understanding Machines

The implementation techniques used in Sound Opinions led to an abnormally large (50k) set of precisely graphed tags. We started using those tags to scheme interesting ways to curate and suggest media—an activity that has never really produced more interesting results than a human. We applied for (and received) a grant from the Knight Foundation to continue those efforts.

The initial work done was able to make simple recommendations, but it was lackluster in a few ways. First, it was slow, and I am of the opinion that caching a dynamic system with what should be dynamic input is somewhat unimpressive. Second, the actual correlation results were obvious (perhaps boring), which was entirely not the goal.

Make Interesting Numbers

A new angle was focused on the recommendation itself, and what input can produce better output. One of the securities of starting from scratch is that you can create information that might be more valuable. A simple experiment was done to feed peculiar input with the explicit intention of fitting an efficient correlation algorithm. This input was for a user was displayed as a colorful gradient and the individual bands colored particular moods or critiques. This strategy solved both problems—it executed faster and produced more interesting results.

This technique is also better than a tagging because it does not conflict with human disposition. I have observed that many people avoid typing certain tags or keywords out of sheer decency. Tagging stories about abuse or cancer on one hand seems disrespectful, and on the other, a set of curated [insert tragedy] pieces is strange thing to expect a user to want. We often use synonyms or keywords as substitutes making the exercise ever more oblique.

An abstract approach works better to compose a meaningful, automated, collection. The fact that human storyteller knows how to negotiate contrasting themes is proof enough that dissimilar plots can fit together in ways that machines cannot identify.

Third Coast

2016

thirdcoastfestival.org

The Third Coast International Audio Festival is an organization beloved by audio-storytelling enthusiasts long before podcasts were a thing.

Design to Size of Client

Third Coast had an archive of audio bundled into a substantial Rails application. They have (still do) a labyrinthian array of audio based content that lives under different contexts and categories. To limit duplication, the relational database had 40+ tables sized over a 1gb and the necessary application code spanned hundreds of application files.

Concerning a gigabyte, perspective helps: the 1911 edition of Encyclopaedia Britannica is 25mb. Based on that, a liberal estimate may argue that a 40-table database contains no more than one encyclopedia’s worth of substantive content. This means the Rails database was ~98% metadata. Needless to say, a large conceptual knot to loosen.

Honor the Knot

All developers start a project with some kind of agenda. Unfortunately, that agenda often dictates the tool or program that will regulate the construction of content which unravels as the framework is tailored to fit the shape of the organization. In this project there was a dizzying array of joins between tables and the very same information modeled and remodeled and re-remodeled. This can be avoided with a graph, and I designed and implemented an application that stored relationships in an xml document. While xml may seem antiquarian, it makes defining object-oriented relationships pretty easy.

This scheme required that all content is structurally identical, meaning the exact same form can be used to describe a person or a story. Each small item is assigned to a model, the application ascribes rules for joining models into relationships, which then model bigger, more complicated ideas. It is perfectly possible to plan an efficient relational database, but the advantage here is one can get started before the entire agenda is ironed out. Projects inevitably evolve and change direction; if extra development resources are available, those resources can be used to evolve and maintain schema. If resources are a bit more precious (which is generally the case in my experience), a graphed dataset is purposefully abstract; changing ideas only requires changing the application code, not how data is stored or accumulates.

Pontificating aside, this xml data document is 2mb. That is ½ of 1% of the original size, or for comparison, the largest ship ever and a tuna fish. More importantly, a robust network was established to organize and navigate content that feels more like a corn maze than a corn field.

Pedagogy

2015

In the age of peak advertising, tracking, trolling and deception, where ought one pontificate re. the internet?

The Digital Information Era

Teaching college, I thought, was going to be an opportunity to take a completely proactive position on interactive design and development. There need not be any protracted discussions of analytics, intersticial ads or Internet Explorer. I was going to muse about the potential of this profound idea (the internet) and students, not yet indoctrinated by a career in corporate tomfoolery, were going to produce work that reminded me that this is a pretty great medium.

This is actually what happens: I introduce myself to a room of distracted and somewhat impatient students and over the next fifteen weeks I do very little musing. I evaluate a lot of work that can best be described as a collage of google queries and questionably outdated techniques (sidebar: I think a class called How to Google would be extremely valuable). The realities of learning and teaching in a world of near-constant distraction, aided by fast and easy access to information, has presented unexpected challenges.

Paradoxically, I understand that my career has involved working on teams designing with an unquestioned goal (not consequence) of producing a statistic like more time on site. I am seeing first-hand the industry's success by observing young people who have been exposed to this effort for most of their lives. Much of my curriculum stresses (enforces) time management because one of the most familiar grievances I hear goes something like I just do not have time left in my schedule for study. I actually believe such remarks to be said in earnest, though in fact, they are patently false. More and more research (and informal class polling) shows that we spend upwards of 5–10 hours a day on our devices entertaining ourselves.

Queue Blooper Sound Effect

It becomes difficult to talk about creating new things when a good chunk of each class is devoted to what we playfully call RTFM material. This can be a bit boring, for sure, but a bigger issue is that little philosophy about the medium or innovative ideas are exchanged, which means everybody loses. An instructor is not an efficient resource for research and problem solving—there is too much to know and too little time. And from the other end, as an instructor, rehashing chapter one week after week is a recipe for burn out.

I should point out this is not a student vs. instructor issue—I believe it is fulfillment vs. [insert distraction]. I think that people want to gain experience, but do not yet realize that the work it takes to get there can be arduous and unappealing. To help bridge that gap, my teaching experiments have led to a merging of some sort of grade-free-alternative school (idealism) with the evaluation methods of a hard-nosed technical academy (rigor). I have written more software to assist those outcomes than I thought I would need to as an instructor. I'll mention, software does not really solve any problems concerned with learning or teaching outcomes, but it does help me understand a student’s process.

Principle One (idealism): under the guidance offered, a student will research, engage and regularly practice material that interests them.
Guided by lengthy outlines, recorded lectures, abstract examples, and single-source research citations
A personalized template is generated for each student, outlining skeleton projects that can simply be filled in with skills gained throughout the semester
The overarching mantra is to provide proof that you are engaged with the material. Prove you don't understand it at all, if you must (a devious trick, I know).
Principle Two (rigor): principle one is mandatory
A web crawler scans student work every night, looking for changes, errors, code quality—essentially any metric that can shine a light on technique.
A dashboard shows the version control metrics, revisions, and critique of that work with a real-time score for the course.

The machine tabulates effort and research. It can't be cajoled into forgiveness or sympathy for lapses in judgment as easily a human (me) can. It is based on cold hard statistics accumulating week after week—sabermetrics for study time. This instrument does not indicate work that is good or bad, but it tells you a nearly bulletproof story about if someone is doing the work it takes to be a successful student.
